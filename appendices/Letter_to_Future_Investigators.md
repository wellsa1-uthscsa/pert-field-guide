# Letter to Future Investigators

Dear Future Investigator,

If you are reading this, you are probably standing near the beginning of a journey.

You may be a medical student working through your first research project and wondering whether you belong in academic medicine. You may be a psychiatry resident trying to understand how research fits into an already demanding clinical career. You may be a graduate student, fellow, or early-career investigator searching for questions worth pursuing.

Whatever brought you here, I am glad you came.

Before you continue on that journey, I want to share a few thoughts that I wish someone had told me earlier.

## Research Is Really About Questions

Research is often introduced through methods. We learn statistics, study design, software, manuscript preparation, presentations, and grant writing. Those skills matter, and over time you will become more comfortable with all of them. What is easier to miss is that none of those things are actually the heart of research.

At its best, research is an organized form of curiosity.

It begins when something about the world feels incomplete and you become unwilling to leave the question alone. A patient does not respond to treatment the way you expected. A pattern keeps appearing in the literature. Two studies disagree with one another. A widely accepted explanation feels insufficient. Somewhere in that moment, curiosity takes hold.

Everything that follows—collecting data, building cohorts, running analyses, writing manuscripts, revising figures, responding to reviewers—is simply an attempt to answer a question that seemed important enough to pursue.

Early in training, it is easy to become focused on projects. Projects feel tangible. They have titles, deadlines, datasets, and deliverables. They appear on CVs and conference programs. They can be completed, submitted, and checked off a list.

Questions are different.

Questions tend to stay with you.

Many investigators eventually discover that the most important thing they possess is not a collection of completed projects but a collection of enduring questions. Some questions disappear once a study is finished. Others continue to surface years later during clinical encounters, conversations with colleagues, journal clubs, or quiet moments when you realize that the explanation you accepted yesterday is no longer sufficient.

Pay attention to those questions.

The questions that continue to capture your attention long after a project ends are often pointing you toward the investigator you are becoming.

When people look at successful scientific careers, they often see publications, grants, awards, and academic titles. What they do not always see is that many of those careers were built around a surprisingly small number of questions pursued patiently over many years.

Projects eventually end. Good questions rarely do.

## Learning to Live With Uncertainty

Most people enter research because they want answers.

That is a reasonable goal. Research does produce answers. What it also produces, often unexpectedly, is a deeper appreciation for uncertainty.

The more closely you examine a problem, the more complicated it becomes. Variables that once seemed straightforward become difficult to define. Findings that initially appeared obvious become harder to interpret. New explanations emerge. Additional questions appear. Entire areas of uncertainty reveal themselves where you previously thought certainty existed.

At first, this can feel frustrating.

You may spend months completing a study only to discover that the most valuable outcome is not the answer you found, but the new questions that emerged along the way.

This is not a sign that the project failed.

It is often a sign that you learned something important.

Good research rarely eliminates uncertainty entirely. More often, it transforms vague uncertainty into better-defined uncertainty. It narrows the range of plausible explanations. It helps us understand which ideas deserve more confidence and which deserve more skepticism. It moves us a little closer to understanding, even when complete understanding remains out of reach.

Learning to be comfortable with that reality is one of the most important transitions in becoming an investigator.

The goal is not to know everything.

The goal is to become increasingly thoughtful about what can and cannot be known.

## Protect Your Integrity and Your Curiosity

Throughout your career, there will be moments when shortcuts seem tempting.

Most of those moments will not look dramatic. They will appear small and easily justified. A conclusion stretched slightly beyond the evidence. A limitation that feels inconvenient to discuss. An unexpected result that does not fit the story you hoped to tell. A citation that probably deserves inclusion but is quietly omitted because it complicates the narrative.

These decisions matter.

Scientific integrity is rarely defined by a single dramatic event. More often, it is built through hundreds of ordinary choices made over years. The habits you develop when no one is watching eventually become your reputation.

Protect that reputation carefully.

A reputation for honesty, fairness, intellectual humility, and thoughtful scholarship is among the most valuable things an investigator can possess. It takes years to build and only moments to damage.

At the same time, protect your curiosity.

Academic careers can become crowded with deadlines, administrative responsibilities, productivity metrics, and external expectations. It is surprisingly easy to lose sight of the questions that drew you into research in the first place.

Whenever that happens, return to the questions.

Return to the things that genuinely fascinate you.

Curiosity is not a luxury in science. It is often the fuel that sustains people through uncertainty, rejected manuscripts, failed analyses, disappointing results, and projects whose outcomes remain far from guaranteed.

## Remember the People Behind the Data

The farther you progress in research, the more time you will spend working with abstractions.

Diagnoses become codes. Patients become observations. Complex human experiences become variables that can be summarized, modeled, and analyzed. After enough time, it becomes surprisingly easy to forget what those data actually represent.

Every observation in a dataset belongs to a person.

Every diagnosis reflects a patient navigating illness.

Every survey response came from someone willing to share part of their experience.

Every genetic sample, laboratory value, and clinical record exists because an individual placed trust in a healthcare system, research institution, or scientific community.

Remembering this changes how research feels.

It encourages humility. It encourages responsibility. It reminds us that the purpose of research is not merely to generate knowledge, but to improve understanding in ways that ultimately benefit people.

The larger and more sophisticated your datasets become, the more important it becomes to remember this simple truth.

Research is about people.

Always.

## Find Good Mentors and Become One

No meaningful scientific career is built alone.

Every investigator is shaped by mentors, colleagues, collaborators, trainees, and friends who help them grow. Some will teach technical skills. Others will teach judgment, resilience, professionalism, or perspective. The most influential mentors often teach things that never appear in formal curricula.

Pay attention to all of them.

Observe not only what successful investigators accomplish, but how they treat people, respond to criticism, handle uncertainty, navigate setbacks, and make decisions when there is no obvious correct answer.

Eventually, you will find yourself mentoring others.

When that time arrives, remember what it felt like to be new.

Remember how confusing research seemed at the beginning. Remember how intimidating conferences, manuscripts, and statistical methods once felt. Remember how much a few words of encouragement could matter.

Share knowledge generously.

Support curiosity wherever you find it.

The influence of a mentor often extends far beyond any publication, grant, or academic accomplishment.

## Science Is a Human Activity

Scientific papers can create the impression that discovery follows a clean and orderly path. Hypotheses are proposed, studies are conducted, results are obtained, and knowledge advances neatly from one step to the next.

Reality is much messier.

Projects fail. Analyses break. Manuscripts are rejected. Grant applications go unfunded. Findings refuse to cooperate with expectations. Entire research programs change direction because of one surprising result.

None of these experiences mean you are failing.

They mean you are participating in science.

The process is often slower, more uncertain, and more complicated than it appears from the outside. Yet it is also more interesting. Some of the most important discoveries emerged because investigators paid attention to observations that did not fit existing explanations. Progress frequently depends on people who remain curious when the story becomes complicated rather than abandoning it.

Science is not a machine that automatically produces truth.

It is a human endeavor carried out by imperfect people attempting to understand an extraordinarily complicated world.

That reality should make us humble, but it should also make us hopeful.

## A Final Thought

If you remember nothing else from this handbook, I hope you remember this:

The purpose of research is not to know everything. No investigator ever will.

The purpose is to understand a little more than we did before and to share that understanding with others.

If this guide has accomplished anything, I hope it has encouraged you to think differently about questions, measurement, evidence, populations, responsibility, and impact. More importantly, I hope it has encouraged you to remain curious.

The world does not need more people who are convinced they already have all the answers.

It needs thoughtful people who are willing to ask better questions, pursue them honestly, and follow the evidence wherever it leads.

Those questions have always been the starting point of discovery.

Wherever your path leads, I hope you continue asking them.

With best wishes for the journey ahead,

**Anne Wells, & The PERT Field Guide Team**
