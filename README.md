# PERT Field Guide

*A Practical Introduction to Psychiatric Epidemiology and Population Health Research*

## Overview

The PERT (Prospective Psychiatry Epidemiology Research Training) Field Guide is an educational handbook designed for medical students, psychiatry residents, graduate students, clinical fellows, and early-career investigators interested in psychiatric epidemiology, population health, and clinical research.

The goal of this guide is not to provide a comprehensive textbook of epidemiology or biostatistics.

Instead, it aims to help trainees develop the habits of thought that characterize effective investigators.

Throughout the handbook, readers are encouraged to think like epidemiologists:

- Ask better questions.
- Define concepts carefully.
- Evaluate evidence critically.
- Consider alternative explanations.
- Think beyond individuals to populations.
- Conduct research responsibly.
- Focus on meaningful impact.

The emphasis is not on memorizing methods.

The emphasis is on developing scientific judgment.

---

## Who This Guide Is For

This handbook was written for:

- Medical students
- Psychiatry residents
- Graduate students
- Clinical fellows
- Early-career investigators

No advanced background in epidemiology, statistics, or population health is required.

Readers should bring curiosity, a willingness to think critically, and an interest in understanding how investigators approach scientific questions.

---

## Educational Philosophy

Most research training emphasizes tools.

Students learn:

- Study designs
- Statistical tests
- Software packages
- Research regulations

These skills are important.

However, technical knowledge alone does not teach someone how to think like an investigator.

The PERT Field Guide focuses on the questions researchers repeatedly ask throughout the lifecycle of a project:

- What makes a good research question?
- What am I actually measuring?
- Should I believe this result?
- What alternative explanations exist?
- Why do health outcomes differ across populations?
- Why does society trust researchers?
- How does research create impact?

These questions form the intellectual framework of the handbook.

## Core Chapters

### Introduction

Introduces epidemiology as a framework for explaining patterns in health and illness and establishes the intellectual structure of the handbook.

### Chapter 1: Asking Better Questions

Explores how observations become research questions and how investigators develop research programs rather than isolated projects.

### Chapter 2: Defining What You Are Studying

Examines measurement, operationalization, validity, reliability, and the challenges of translating concepts into variables.

### Chapter 3: Should You Believe the Result?

Introduces causal thinking, confounding, bias, reverse causation, healthcare utilization, and critical interpretation of evidence.

### Chapter 4: Thinking Like a Population Health Researcher

Expands the focus from individuals to populations, emphasizing social determinants of health, health disparities, life-course perspectives, and upstream causes.

### Chapter 5: Research, Responsibility, and Trust

Explores ethics, stewardship, representation, transparency, scientific integrity, and the social contract underlying research.

### Chapter 6: From Question to Impact

Examines how scientific findings become knowledge, influence practice, shape policy, and contribute to long-term research programs.

## Guiding Principle

The purpose of this handbook is not to teach everything.

The purpose is to help readers think more clearly about questions, evidence, populations, responsibility, and impact.

When uncertainty arises, the handbook favors clarity over completeness and judgment over memorization.
