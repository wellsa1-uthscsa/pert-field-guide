# Introduction

## Why This Guide Exists

Most people are introduced to research in pieces.

One lecture focuses on study design. Another introduces statistical tests. A journal club teaches critical appraisal. A mentor demonstrates how to analyze a dataset. An online training module explains research ethics. Each component is useful, and each represents an important part of the research process. Yet many trainees finish their early research experiences with the same feeling: they have learned a collection of techniques without ever being taught how investigators actually think.

This guide was written to connect those pieces.

The PERT Field Guide is not intended to be a traditional epidemiology textbook, nor is it a comprehensive review of biostatistics, study design, or research methods. Excellent resources already exist for those purposes. Instead, this handbook focuses on the questions that repeatedly emerge throughout the life of a research project. Whether an investigator is studying depression, Alzheimer's disease, cardiovascular risk, public health policy, neurodevelopment, or healthcare systems, they often find themselves returning to the same fundamental uncertainties.

How do observations become research questions? How do we decide what we are actually measuring? When should we believe an association, and when should we remain skeptical? Why do health outcomes differ across populations? Why does society place trust in researchers, and how does research ultimately create impact?

The methods used to answer those questions may change over time. Software platforms will come and go. Datasets will evolve. New technologies will emerge. The questions themselves, however, remain remarkably consistent.

The purpose of this handbook is not to teach everything. That would be impossible. Instead, it aims to help readers develop habits of thought that remain useful long after specific methods, software packages, and datasets have changed.

## Why Epidemiology Matters

Epidemiology is often defined as the study of the distribution and determinants of health and disease in populations. The definition is accurate, but it can also feel abstract, especially to students encountering the field for the first time.

A more useful way to think about epidemiology is as the science of explaining patterns.

Why do some individuals develop disease while others do not? Why do health outcomes differ across communities, healthcare systems, or countries? Why do certain risk factors repeatedly appear across studies? Why do some interventions succeed in one setting but fail in another?

These questions all begin with observations. Something appears unevenly distributed. A pattern emerges. An investigator notices that the pattern exists and begins searching for an explanation.

Contrary to popular perception, epidemiology is not limited to outbreaks or infectious disease. Some of the most influential discoveries in modern medicine emerged from epidemiologic thinking. Researchers identified the relationship between smoking and lung cancer long before the biological mechanisms were fully understood. Public health investigators demonstrated the importance of clean water systems, transforming health outcomes for entire populations. Large cohort studies clarified major cardiovascular risk factors and reshaped preventive medicine.

In each case, the process began in the same way: someone noticed a pattern and asked why it existed.

The ability to identify meaningful patterns and evaluate competing explanations remains one of the most powerful tools in medicine.

## Why Psychiatry Needs Epidemiology

Psychiatry presents unique challenges for researchers. Many psychiatric conditions lack definitive laboratory tests. Symptoms fluctuate over time, diagnoses are often based on clinical assessment, and outcomes are influenced by a complicated mixture of biological, psychological, social, and environmental factors.

As a result, many psychiatric questions extend beyond individual patients.

Why are depression rates increasing among certain populations? Why do mental health outcomes differ across communities? Why do some individuals recover while others remain symptomatic despite receiving similar treatments? Why do social and economic conditions appear to exert such powerful effects on psychiatric health?

These are fundamentally epidemiologic questions.

Understanding mental health requires understanding populations. It requires examining how risk, resilience, illness, and recovery are distributed across groups of people and exploring the factors that shape those patterns. Psychiatric epidemiology provides a framework for approaching those questions systematically, allowing investigators to move beyond anecdote and toward evidence.

For this reason, epidemiologic thinking has become increasingly important within modern psychiatry, particularly as large-scale datasets create new opportunities to study mental health at unprecedented scale.

## A Question We Will Return To

Throughout this handbook, we will revisit a single observation several times: individuals who experience chronic sleep disturbance often appear to be at greater risk for depression. The example itself is not especially important. In fact, this handbook is not really about sleep, and it is certainly not intended to be a handbook about depression. The reason the example appears repeatedly is that it provides a useful way to demonstrate how investigators approach the same problem from different perspectives.

A clinician might view the observation as a patient-care question. An epidemiologist might view it as a problem of measurement and study design. A population health researcher might ask why the relationship appears more strongly in some groups than others. Later, questions about confounding, causality, ethics, communication, and impact begin to emerge. The underlying observation remains the same, but the questions surrounding it continue to evolve.

One of the central lessons of research is that important discoveries rarely come from finding a single answer. More often, they come from learning how to ask increasingly sophisticated questions about the same phenomenon. Following a single example through multiple chapters allows us to practice that way of thinking as we move through the handbook.

## The Rise of Population-Scale Data

For much of medical history, investigators worked with relatively small studies. Data collection was labor-intensive, follow-up was often difficult, and answering even straightforward questions could require years of effort. Many important discoveries emerged from those studies, but researchers were frequently constrained by the amount of information they could realistically collect and analyze.

Today, the landscape looks very different. Investigators have access to resources that previous generations could scarcely imagine: electronic health records, national registries, biobanks, claims databases, wearable devices, and large-scale surveys that collectively contain information from hundreds of thousands—or even millions—of individuals. Questions that were once impossible to study can now be addressed with increasing precision, and rare conditions that previously required multi-center collaborations may be identifiable within a single dataset.

These developments have transformed research, but they have not changed its fundamental challenges.

Large datasets can reveal patterns that would otherwise remain hidden, yet they can also generate an overwhelming number of associations. Modern software makes it increasingly easy to identify statistical relationships. Determining whether those relationships are meaningful, causal, clinically important, or merely artifacts of bias and confounding remains just as difficult as it has always been.

In some ways, the growth of population-scale data has made epidemiologic thinking more important rather than less. When almost anyone can generate a result, the ability to critically evaluate that result becomes increasingly valuable.

The central challenge of epidemiology has never been finding patterns.

The central challenge is deciding which explanations deserve our confidence.

## How the Chapters Fit Together

The chapters that follow are organized around a sequence of questions that investigators repeatedly encounter throughout the research process. Although the chapters can be read independently, they are designed to build upon one another and reflect the progression from curiosity to impact.

The framework is intentionally simple. Research rarely follows a perfectly linear path, and investigators often move back and forth between these stages. Nevertheless, the sequence captures many of the core challenges that emerge as a project develops.

### Chapter 1: Asking Better Questions

Research begins long before data collection. It begins when an observation captures someone's attention and generates curiosity. The first chapter explores how investigators transform observations into research questions and how those questions can eventually evolve into broader research programs.

At its core, the chapter asks:

> What should I study?

### Chapter 2: Defining What You Are Studying

Before a question can be answered, it must be translated into something measurable. This process is often more difficult than it first appears. Concepts such as depression, resilience, quality of life, social support, and recovery feel intuitive, yet each can be defined in multiple ways.

The second chapter examines how investigators move from concepts to variables and why those choices matter.

The central question is:

> What am I actually measuring?

### Chapter 3: Should You Believe the Result?

Finding an association is not the same thing as finding an explanation.

This chapter introduces some of the most important ideas in epidemiology, including confounding, bias, reverse causation, and causal reasoning. More importantly, it explores the mindset of a skeptical investigator who actively searches for alternative explanations before accepting a conclusion.

The central question is:

> Should I believe this result?

### Chapter 4: Thinking Like a Population Health Researcher

Many health outcomes cannot be fully understood at the level of individual patients alone. Health emerges within families, communities, healthcare systems, economies, and policy environments that shape risk and opportunity long before symptoms appear.

This chapter expands the lens from individuals to populations and introduces the habit of asking what broader forces might be influencing the patterns we observe.

The central question is:

> Why does this pattern exist across populations?

### Chapter 5: Research, Responsibility, and Trust

Research depends on trust. Participants trust investigators. Communities trust institutions. Society trusts scientists to communicate findings honestly and use information responsibly.

This chapter examines the responsibilities that accompany scientific inquiry and explores why ethics is not separate from research but woven throughout every stage of the process.

The central question is:

> Why should society trust this work?

### Chapter 6: From Question to Impact

Research does not end when an analysis is complete or when a manuscript is published. Findings become part of a larger scientific conversation that influences future studies, clinical practice, public health, education, and policy.

The final chapter explores how individual projects contribute to broader research programs and how scientific work ultimately creates impact.

The central question is:

> What happens next?

## How to Use This Guide

This handbook was written primarily for medical students, psychiatry residents, graduate students, and early-career investigators. No advanced training in epidemiology or biostatistics is required. Whenever possible, technical concepts are introduced through examples and practical questions rather than formal mathematical explanations.

Although the chapters build upon one another, readers should feel free to revisit sections as needed. Many investigators find that concepts they initially encountered as beginners take on new meaning after they have completed a few projects of their own. Questions that once seemed theoretical often become immediately relevant when faced with real data, real patients, and real uncertainty.

The goal of this handbook is not to provide an exhaustive education in research methods. Entire careers can be spent mastering epidemiology, biostatistics, causal inference, or population health. Instead, the goal is to provide a framework for thinking about research that remains useful regardless of discipline, dataset, or career stage.

Specific methods will change.

Software platforms will change.

Datasets will change.

The habits of mind that allow investigators to ask good questions and evaluate evidence thoughtfully are much more durable.

## A Different Way of Thinking

Many educational programs emphasize answers. Research often begins with uncertainty.

The most successful investigators are not necessarily those who memorize the most facts or become experts in the largest number of statistical techniques. More often, they are the people who remain curious longer than everyone else. They notice patterns that others overlook, become uncomfortable with incomplete explanations, and continue asking questions after others have moved on.

Over time, these habits begin to shape how they see the world. Clinical encounters become opportunities for observation. Unexpected findings become opportunities for learning. Contradictions become invitations to investigate rather than problems to avoid.

The chapters that follow are ultimately about developing that mindset.

Research does not begin with a dataset.

It does not begin with a statistical test.

It does not begin with a manuscript.

It begins when someone notices something interesting about the world and becomes curious enough to ask why.

The first chapter begins with the foundation of every research project:

A question.
